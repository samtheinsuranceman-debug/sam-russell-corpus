## Abstract

This report presents a computational analysis of 6,346 chess games played by a single subject across two platforms and multiple game variants over a 14-month period (August 2024 – October 2025). The subject simultaneously maintained a parallel practice of improvisational freestyle rap and self-directed hypnotic induction, documented across 26 audio recordings spanning 2019–2024. By cross-referencing performance data across these domains, we identify a consistent cognitive signature: the subject demonstrates dramatically superior performance in divergent, expanding-possibility-space tasks (Crazy House chess, freestyle rap) compared to convergent, narrowing-possibility-space tasks (standard chess). Furthermore, performance in divergent tasks improves under conditions of conscious fatigue, suggesting a cognitive architecture in which the analytical prefrontal system acts as an inhibitor rather than a facilitator of the subject's primary processing mode. These findings have implications for understanding individual differences in cognitive style, the neuroscience of flow states, and the design of personalized cognitive training protocols.

## 1. Introduction: Two Games, One Brain, Radically Different Outcomes

Standard chess and Crazy House chess share a board, pieces, and basic movement rules. To a casual observer, they appear to be the same game with a minor variation. To a cognitive scientist, they are fundamentally different tasks that recruit different neural architectures.

In standard chess, captured pieces are removed permanently. The game begins with maximum complexity and narrows toward simplicity. Each exchange reduces the possibility space. The optimal strategy is convergent: calculate the best line among a diminishing set of options. Grandmasters describe this as "pruning the decision tree" — a process that rewards systematic, sequential, analytical thinking rooted in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex [1].

In Crazy House, captured pieces switch sides and can be reintroduced anywhere on the board. The game begins with standard complexity and *expands* with each exchange. Every capture creates new offensive and defensive possibilities for both players simultaneously. The possibility space grows exponentially rather than shrinking linearly. There is no "pruning" — there is only rapid pattern recognition across an ever-expanding field of options [2].

The subject of this study, a 42-year-old male with no formal chess training, achieved a peak rating of **1608** in Crazy House while simultaneously maintaining a peak rating of only **391** in standard chess on a separate platform. This four-fold differential cannot be explained by experience alone (he played 7.5x more standard games than Crazy House games). It represents a fundamental cognitive asymmetry that, when examined alongside his parallel freestyle rap practice, reveals a coherent theory of individual cognitive architecture.
